# A Framework for Deciding Under Uncertainty

## The Paralysis of Not Knowing

You're facing a decision. Not the trivial kind—what to eat for dinner—but the consequential kind. Career pivot. Relationship crossroads. Investment of time and money you can't get back.

The information you need doesn't exist. Or it exists but you can't access it. Or you can access it but you don't know which parts to trust.

Welcome to decision-making under uncertainty. Most people handle it poorly. They either freeze (analysis paralysis) or rush (impulsive choices they'll regret). There's a better way.

---

## The Core Problem: Confusing Uncertainty with Risk

Risk is calculable. You know the probabilities. A coin flip. A diversified portfolio. Insurance actuarial tables.

Uncertainty is not calculable. You don't know what you don't know. Emerging markets. New relationships. Creative endeavors.

**Most people treat uncertainty like risk.** They want spreadsheets, percentages, confidence intervals. These tools fail when the underlying distribution is unknown.

**The framework:** When facing uncertainty, optimize for optionality and reversible commitment.

---

## Part 1: The Optionality Test

Optionality means preserving future choices. Not maximizing them—that's paralysis—but preserving them.

**Ask:** Does this decision increase or decrease my future options?

**High optionality:** Learning a skill, building a network, saving money, maintaining health.

**Low optionality:** Burning bridges, signing non-competes, over-specializing in dying fields.

This sounds obvious until you notice how often people sacrifice optionality for short-term comfort. The stable job that teaches nothing transferable. The relationship that isolates you from friends. The "practical" degree in a field you hate.

**The trap:** Optionality feels like anxiety (too many choices) rather than freedom. Learn to recognize the difference.

---

## Part 2: The Reversibility Filter

Some decisions are reversible. Others are not. Most people spend equal energy on both.

**Ask:** If this goes wrong, how hard is it to undo?

**Easily reversible:** Taking a new job (can quit), trying a new city (can move), starting a side project (can stop).

**Nearly irreversible:** Having children, major surgery, committing crimes, burning professional bridges.

**Allocate decision-making resources accordingly.** Spend 20% of your energy on reversible decisions, 80% on irreversible ones.

For reversible decisions: Bias toward action. The cost of being wrong is low. The cost of waiting is high (opportunity cost, regret, stagnation).

For irreversible decisions: Slow down. Gather more information. Seek diverse perspectives. Sleep on it.

---

## Part 3: The Information Value Calculation

Before gathering more information, ask: Will this change my decision?

**Calculate:**
- Cost of acquiring information (time, money, opportunity)
- Probability it changes your conclusion
- Magnitude of improvement if it does change

**Example:** You're deciding between Job A and Job B. You could spend 3 days researching Company B's culture in depth. Will this change your decision? If the culture is great, you take B. If terrible, you take A. But you already know the culture is "generally good." Unless you uncover something shocking, you'll still take B. The information has low value.

**Exception:** Irreversible decisions. For these, information gathering has asymmetric value. The downside of missing something is catastrophic. Spend more.

---

## Part 4: The Premortem

Before committing, imagine it's one year later and this decision failed spectacularly. What went wrong?

**This isn't pessimism.** It's structured imagination. Most people are biased toward optimism (planning fallacy). The premortem corrects this.

**Ask:**
- What was the most likely failure mode?
- What warning signs did I ignore?
- What would have prevented this?

**Then:** Implement the preventions if they're cheap, or build detection for the warning signs.

**Example:** Starting a business. Premortem reveals: "I ran out of runway because I underestimated customer acquisition costs." Prevention: Secure 18 months of runway instead of 6. Detection: Track CAC obsessively from day one.

---

## Part 5: The 10-10-10 Rule

**Ask:** How will I feel about this decision in 10 minutes, 10 months, and 10 years?

**10 minutes:** Emotional reaction. Excitement? Fear? Social pressure? This is usually noise.

**10 months:** Practical consequences. Will this still matter? Will I have adapted?

**10 years:** Identity impact. Will this change who I become?

**Most bad decisions overweight the 10-minute feelings and underweight the 10-year consequences.**

The job that looks impressive (10 minutes) but teaches you nothing (10 years). The argument you win (10 minutes) that destroys a relationship (10 years).

---

## Putting It Together: The Decision Matrix

For any uncertain decision, run through these five filters:

| Filter | Question | Action if Unclear |
|--------|----------|-------------------|
| **Optionality** | Does this preserve future options? | Choose the more flexible path |
| **Reversibility** | How hard to undo? | If reversible, act faster |
| **Information Value** | Will more data change my choice? | If no, decide now |
| **Premortem** | How could this fail? | Build preventions/detectors |
| **10-10-10** | How will I feel at each horizon? | Don't overweight short-term |

**You don't need perfect answers.** You need directionally correct answers applied consistently.

---

## When This Framework Fails

**Emotionally charged decisions:** Grief, anger, infatuation. The framework helps, but you may need to wait for emotional clarity.

**Ethical decisions:** The framework optimizes for your outcomes. Ethics may require sacrifice. Don't use this to rationalize harm.

**No good options:** Sometimes all paths are bad. The framework helps you pick the least bad, but it won't make it good.

---

## The Meta-Decision

The most important decision is deciding *how* you decide.

Most people use the same decision process for everything: gut feeling, plus whatever information is immediately available, plus social pressure.

Better: Match the decision process to the decision stakes.

**Low stakes, reversible:** Gut feeling is fine. Decide quickly.

**Medium stakes, some reversibility:** Use the framework. Spend an hour.

**High stakes, irreversible:** Full analysis. Consult experts. Premortem. 10-10-10. Sleep on it. Decide slowly.

**The cost of over-optimizing low-stakes decisions:** You exhaust your decision-making energy for when it matters.

---

## The Practice

Decision-making is a skill. Like all skills, it improves with deliberate practice.

**This week:** Notice one uncertain decision you face. Run it through the five filters. Don't necessarily change your choice—just notice what the framework reveals.

**This month:** Keep a decision journal. Write down the decision, what you chose, and why. Review in 6 months. Calibrate.

**This year:** Notice patterns in your decision failures. Do you consistently overweight social proof? Underweight reversibility? Ignore premortems? Fix the pattern, not just the decision.

---

## Summary

Uncertainty isn't failure of information. It's the default state of important decisions.

Don't chase certainty you can't have. Build a decision process that works with uncertainty:

1. **Preserve optionality** — Keep future choices open
2. **Respect reversibility** — Act fast on reversible, slow on permanent
3. **Value information correctly** — Don't research what won't change your mind
4. **Premortem failures** — Imagine what went wrong before it does
5. **Use 10-10-10** — Don't let 10-minute feelings drive 10-year consequences

The goal isn't perfect decisions. It's *better* decisions, made with less anxiety, more clarity, and appropriate speed.

Your future self—the one living with the consequences—is watching. Decide accordingly.

## Short-Form Note 2: Goal Allocation (287 words)

Everyone talks about goal-setting. Nobody talks about goal allocation.

We obsess over which stock to pick—Target or Walmart?—when we should be asking: what's my asset allocation?

Individual stock selection is fun. It feels like control. But research is clear: asset allocation (the ratio between stocks, bonds, commodities, cash) drives 90%+ of portfolio performance.

Goals work the same way.

Comparing "get promoted" to "get a raise" is like comparing Target to Walmart. Same category, similar outcome. You're optimizing within consumer staples.

But what about Target vs. Nvidia? vs. oil futures? vs. prediction markets?

Now you're talking different risk profiles, different industries, different economic sensitivities. The outcome space widens dramatically.

**The goal allocation framework:**

1. **System vs. Self** (50/50? 70/30?)
   - System goals: Serve others, automatic/ scalable
   - Self goals: Personal growth, require your presence

2. **Automatic vs. Manual** (within each)
   - Automatic: Run in background, compound over time
   - Manual: Require active focus, immediate returns

3. **Industry diversification**
   - Career, relationships, health, wealth, creativity
   - Don't let one category dominate

4. **Risk profile**
   - Conservative goals (stable, reliable)
   - Growth goals (uncertain, asymmetric upside)
   - Speculative goals (high risk, lottery ticket)

Most people have a portfolio of 90% "career goals"—all correlated, all tank in a recession.

Build a balanced portfolio. Rebalance quarterly.

The goal isn't to pick the right goal. It's to construct the right mix.

## Short-Form Note 3: Wu Wei and the River (276 words)

Taoism has a concept called *wu wei*—"effortless action" or "non-doing."

It's not laziness. It's alignment.

The metaphor: a river flows downhill. You can swim upstream (exhausting, futile) or you can recognize the current and accelerate along it.

Modern productivity culture teaches the opposite: hustle harder, willpower through, optimize every minute.

But what if you're optimizing the wrong direction?

**First, recognize which way your river flows.**

What are you naturally good at? What energizes you? What would you do even if unpaid? That's your river.

**Then, set goals that accelerate along that river.**

Not across it (resistance). Not against it (exhaustion). Along it (momentum).

This is why "you can just do things" is both liberating and terrifying. Without the river's direction, you can paddle anywhere—and end up nowhere.

The wisdom traditions converge here:
- Stoicism: Focus on what you control (your direction), accept what you don't (the current)
- Buddhism: Attachment to specific outcomes creates suffering; flow with change
- Taoism: Wu wei—effortless action in alignment with natural patterns

**The modern application:**

AI is the current. It's flowing toward automation, democratization, individual empowerment.

You can fight it (obsolete skills, resentment) or you can recognize it and accelerate along it.

Set goals that:
1. Align with your natural strengths (the river)
2. Serve others (enrich lives along the way)
3. Leverage the current (AI-augmented, not AI-replaced)

The question isn't "what can I do?"

It's "what will I do, in this direction, with this current, for these people?"

## The Shift Nobody's Talking About

Until recently, your path was preset. If you were born into a rural family, you worked the land. Born into a Catholic family, you went to church every Sunday. Born into a suburban middle-class family, you worked hard in school to get a higher-paying job with an easier life than your parents had.

Incremental progress, sure. But within a limited opportunity set.

Now? The constraint has moved from *access* to *wisdom*.

The barrier is no longer social class or education or capital. The barrier is deciding which of the nearly infinite paths to choose.

From capability to wisdom. From "can I?" to "should I?"

This should be liberating. For many, it's paralyzing.

---

## The Paradox of Infinite Choice

Psychologists call it the paradox of choice: more options don't make us happier. They make us anxious. We fear missing out on the road not taken. We second-guess. We optimize for the wrong variables.

I see this everywhere. Smart, capable people spending their days deciding between Target and Walmart—comparing two consumer staples stocks with nearly identical risk profiles—when they should be questioning whether they want to be in retail at all.

What about Target vs. Nvidia? Now you're comparing industries, growth profiles, economic sensitivities. The outcome space widens.

But even that misses the point.

What about stocks vs. bonds? vs. commodities? vs. derivatives? vs. prediction markets?

The individual stock selection—the specific goal you set—is less important than your asset allocation. The mix. The ratio between categories.

Goals work the same way.

---

## Goal Allocation: The Framework

Everyone talks about goal-setting. Nobody talks about goal allocation.

Financial research is clear: asset allocation drives 90%+ of portfolio performance. Not stock-picking. Not market timing. The ratio between stocks, bonds, cash, commodities, and alternatives.

Your life portfolio deserves the same discipline.

**Dimension 1: System vs. Self**

System goals serve others. They're often scalable, automatic, infrastructure-building. The things you "have to do" to keep the world running.

Self goals serve your growth. They require your personal time, presence, attention. The things you "want to do" as an individual.

What's your ratio? 50/50? 70/30? Most people drift into 90/10 System without realizing it—then wonder why they feel hollow.

**Dimension 2: Automatic vs. Manual**

Within each category, distinguish between:
- Automatic: Run in background, compound over time, require minimal maintenance
- Manual: Require active focus, immediate returns, consume attention

The magic is in the automatic. Set up systems that serve goals while you sleep.

**Dimension 3: Industry Diversification**

Don't let one category dominate. Career, relationships, health, wealth, creativity, community—these are different industries of your life. They respond to different economic conditions. They hedge each other.

A portfolio of 90% "career goals" is like owning only tech stocks. Great in bull markets. Devastating in corrections.

**Dimension 4: Risk Profile**

- Conservative goals: Stable, reliable, likely to succeed
- Growth goals: Uncertain, asymmetric upside, worth the variance
- Speculative goals: High risk, lottery tickets, small allocation only

Rebalance quarterly. Just like a financial portfolio.

Not just any things. The right things.

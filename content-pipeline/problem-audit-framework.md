# The Problem Audit Framework
## A Diagnostic System for Identifying, Validating, and Solving the Right Problems

**Version:** 1.0  
**Date:** February 24, 2026  
**Framework Type:** Diagnostic / Decision Support  
**Time to Apply:** 15-30 minutes for initial audit; ongoing refinement

---

## The Premise

Most people solve symptoms. They treat the cough instead of the infection, the calendar overflow instead of the prioritization failure, the low revenue instead of the wrong market fit.

The Problem Audit Framework is a causal identification system. It moves you from "what hurts" through "why it hurts" to "what would actually fix it."

**Core Principle:** The right problem, poorly solved, beats the wrong problem, perfectly solved.

---

## Phase I: The Entry Point — Five Diagnostic Questions

These questions function as triage. They reveal where your energy is going and whether it's going to the right place.

### Question 1: The Target Assessment
**"Are you solving the right problem?"**

Most problems are actually solutions to deeper problems you haven't named yet. You're fighting fires when the real issue is your electrical wiring.

**When to ask:** When you're exhausted but not making progress. When you've "solved" this three times and it keeps coming back.

**What it surfaces:** Whether your current focus is a symptom or a root cause.

---

### Question 2: The Validation Check
**"How do you know if you're solving the right problem?"**

Evidence beats intuition. This question forces you to articulate your validation criteria—how you would recognize the right problem if you saw it.

**When to ask:** Before committing significant resources. When you're operating on assumptions.

**What it surfaces:** Whether you have observable criteria for success, or just hope.

---

### Question 3: The Completion Criteria
**"How do you know when the problem has been solved?"**

If you can't define "done," you'll never be done. This question reveals whether you have a finish line or just a direction.

**When to ask:** When projects drag on indefinitely. When "success" keeps moving.

**What it surfaces:** Whether you have clear success criteria or scope creep waiting to happen.

---

### Question 4: The Agency Audit
**"Are you spending time doing what you want to do? Or what you have to do?"**

This cuts to the heart of agency. "Have to" tasks are constraints; "want to" tasks are choices. The ratio between them determines whether you're living your life or managing it.

**When to ask:** When you feel trapped by your own calendar. When you dread your own to-do list.

**What it surfaces:** The gap between your stated priorities and your actual allocation of attention.

---

### Question 5: The Constraint Examination
**"Why 'must you' do what you 'have to' do? What are the impacts of not doing?"**

Not all "have tos" are real. This question separates true constraints from inherited obligations, social expectations, and unexamined assumptions.

**When to ask:** When you feel obligated but not committed. When you're maintaining systems that no longer serve you.

**What it surfaces:** Which constraints are negotiable, and what freedom you actually have.

## Phase II: The Causal Identification Process

Once you've surfaced a candidate problem through the five questions, apply this three-layer analysis to determine if you've found a root cause or just another symptom.

### Layer 1: Surface Problems (Symptoms)
**Characteristics:** Immediate, visible, painful  
**Examples:** Missed deadline, low sales, conflict with colleague, exhaustion

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- What specifically is not working?
- When did it start?
- Who is affected?
- How often does it occur?

**Trap:** Solving here provides temporary relief but the problem regenerates.

---

### Layer 2: Contributing Factors (Proximate Causes)
**Characteristics:** Explanatory, conditional, systemic  
**Examples:** Poor planning process, unclear priorities, skill gap, resource constraint

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- What conditions allow this symptom to exist?
- What would have to be different for this not to happen?
- Is this a one-time failure or a pattern?
- What systems or processes are involved?

**Trap:** Solving here improves outcomes but underlying dynamics persist.

---

### Layer 3: Root Causes (Fundamental Dynamics)
**Characteristics:** Generative, upstream, structural  
**Examples:** Identity-level beliefs about success/failure, organizational culture, market positioning, life design

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- What belief or assumption makes the contributing factors acceptable?
- What would need to be true at a fundamental level for this to resolve completely?
- What are we unwilling to question or change?
- What does this problem allow us to avoid?

**Opportunity:** Solving here eliminates the problem category, not just the instance.

---

## Phase III: The Validation Protocol

Before committing to a solution, validate that you've identified the right problem at the right layer.

### The 5-Why Test
Ask "why" five times, moving from symptom toward cause:

**Example:**
1. Why am I exhausted? → Working 60-hour weeks
2. Why am I working 60-hour weeks? → Taking on too many projects
3. Why am I taking on too many projects? → Can't say no to opportunities
4. Why can't I say no? → Fear of missing the "one" that succeeds
5. Why the fear? → Belief that success requires catching lucky breaks, not building systems

**Root cause:** Belief about how success works → drives overcommitment → drives exhaustion

---

### The Reverse Test
Ask: "If I solved this completely, what would be different?"

- **If nothing else changes** → You're solving a symptom
- **If related problems also resolve** → You're solving a contributing factor  
- **If your entire relationship to this domain shifts** → You're solving a root cause

---

### The Substitution Test
Ask: "What else could cause this same symptom?"

If multiple root causes could produce your surface problem, you need more data before committing to a solution.

---

## Phase IV: The Solution Mapping

Once you've identified the right problem at the right layer, match your intervention to the layer.

| Problem Layer | Solution Type | Timeframe | Investment Level |
|---------------|---------------|-----------|------------------|
| **Surface (Symptom)** | Tactical fix | Immediate | Low |
| **Contributing (Proximate)** | Process/system change | Weeks | Medium |
| **Root (Fundamental)** | Identity/culture/strategy shift | Months+ | High |

### Critical Rule
**Never solve a root cause problem with a surface solution.**

You can't belief-change your way out of a cash flow crisis (solve the symptom first).  
You can't process-improve your way out of a market-positioning problem (solve the root eventually).

---

## Phase V: The Audit Output

A complete Problem Audit produces:

### 1. Problem Statement (One sentence)
"The fundamental problem is [root cause], which manifests as [surface symptoms] and is sustained by [contributing factors]."

**Example:**  
"The fundamental problem is a belief that success requires heroic effort, which manifests as 60-hour weeks and exhaustion, and is sustained by poor boundary-setting and opportunity FOMO."

---

### 2. Validation Criteria (Observable)
"We will know this problem is solved when [specific, measurable condition]."

**Example:**  
"We will know this is solved when I consistently work 40-hour weeks while maintaining output, and can articulate why I decline opportunities."

---

### 3. Intervention Strategy (Layer-matched)
- **Immediate (Surface):** [Tactical fix]
- **Short-term (Contributing):** [Process change]
- **Long-term (Root):** [Belief/identity work]

**Example:**
- **Immediate:** Block non-negotiable rest time; use Problem Audit on all new opportunities
- **Short-term:** Implement 5 Filters for opportunity evaluation; build "no" templates
- **Long-term:** Examine success beliefs; gather evidence for systems-based vs. luck-based outcomes

---

### 4. Risk Assessment
"If we solve this problem, what else might change? What might break?"

**Example:**  
"May disappoint people used to my availability. May slow growth short-term. May force confrontation with identity as 'hard worker.'"

## Common Traps

**The Urgency Trap:** Solving surface problems because they're loud, not because they're root causes.

**The Complexity Trap:** Believing root causes must be complicated. Often they're embarrassingly simple.

**The Blame Trap:** Looking for who caused the problem rather than what system allows it.

**The Solution Trap:** Falling in love with a solution before properly identifying the problem.

---

## Success Indicators

You'll know the Problem Audit Framework is working when:
- You catch yourself solving symptoms and pause
- Problems stop regenerating after you "fix" them
- Your energy shifts from managing crises to building systems
- You can articulate why you're *not* working on something
